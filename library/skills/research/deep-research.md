# Deep Research

A disciplined approach to researching complex or unfamiliar topics across multiple sources.

## Workflow

1. **Frame** — turn the question into specific, answerable sub-questions.
2. **Gather** — collect sources deliberately, not opportunistically.
3. **Evaluate** — weigh source quality and relevance.
4. **Triangulate** — cross-check claims across sources.
5. **Synthesize** — build a coherent model with cited evidence.
6. **Identify gaps** — note what you couldn't verify.

## Framing the Question

Vague questions produce vague research. Break down until each sub-question:
- Has a definable answer type (number, comparison, mechanism).
- Is narrow enough to research in one session.
- Maps to search terms or knowable sources.

"Is tool X good?" → "What are tool X's stated guarantees? Who uses it in production at what scale? What's the failure mode reported in incidents? How does latency compare to Y?"

## Source Diversity

Rely on multiple source types:
- **Primary** — specs, source code, vendor docs, original research.
- **Secondary** — technical blogs, conference talks, case studies.
- **Tertiary** — surveys, overviews, Wikipedia for orientation.

Weight primary sources higher. Tertiary is a starting point, not a conclusion.

## Source Evaluation

For each source ask:
- Who wrote it? What's their expertise and bias?
- When was it written? Still current?
- Is it backed by evidence or assertion?
- Does it contradict other credible sources? Why?

Discard confidently-asserted but unsupported claims.

## Triangulation

Never rely on a single source for a factual claim. Look for:
- At least two independent sources agreeing.
- Original source behind secondary claims.
- Counter-evidence actively sought out.

When sources disagree, investigate the disagreement — it often reveals nuance.

## Note-Taking

- Capture quotes and citations verbatim.
- Record the source URL, author, date.
- Summarize in your own words immediately.
- Track open questions as you go.

## Synthesis

- Organize by topic, not by source.
- Present the model, then the evidence.
- Cite claims inline with specific sources.
- Distinguish consensus from minority views.
- State uncertainty explicitly.

## Identifying Gaps

After synthesis, surface:
- Claims you couldn't verify.
- Contradictions left unresolved.
- Areas where sources were scarce or thin.
- Next questions the research opened.

## Time Management

- Box time per question; research is infinite.
- Go depth-first on the load-bearing question, breadth on the rest.
- Cheap, fast orientation first; expensive deep-dives only where needed.

## Common Pitfalls

- Confirmation bias — stopping when you find what you expected.
- Single-source dependence.
- Conflating recency with accuracy.
- Skipping primary sources because secondary is easier.
- Over-researching; failing to conclude.

## Output Format

A deep research output includes:
- Executive summary (1–3 paragraphs).
- Key findings with citations.
- Open questions and gaps.
- Sources with annotations (why each was useful or limited).
