# Market Research

A framework for investigating markets, customers, competitors, and positioning.

## Core Questions

Every market study should answer:
1. Who are the customers?
2. What are they hiring the product to do?
3. What alternatives do they already use?
4. What's underserved?
5. How big is the opportunity?
6. What would make someone switch?

## Customer Segmentation

- Segment by problem, not demographics when possible.
- Identify the **job to be done** — what outcome do they want?
- Distinguish user (who operates) from buyer (who pays) from beneficiary.
- Size each segment: count, spend, growth.

## Customer Research Methods

- **Interviews** — open-ended, focused on past behavior, not hypotheticals.
- **Surveys** — quantify what interviews surface qualitatively.
- **Analytics** — observe actual behavior, not stated intent.
- **Public artifacts** — reviews, support forums, job listings, GitHub issues.

Prefer evidence of action over expressed opinion.

## Competitive Analysis

For each competitor:
- What segments do they serve?
- What's their value proposition?
- What do customers praise in reviews? Complain about?
- Pricing model and public pricing.
- Growth signals (hiring, funding, press).
- Weaknesses — gaps you could exploit.

Don't just list features. Understand why customers choose them.

## Market Sizing

Triangulate:
- **Top-down** — industry reports, public company filings.
- **Bottom-up** — customer count × ACV or transaction count × fee.
- **Analogous** — similar markets scaled by relevant multipliers.

Disagreement between methods signals assumptions to re-examine.

## Trends and Forces

Scan for:
- Regulatory shifts.
- Technology changes enabling new solutions.
- Distribution/platform shifts.
- Demographic or macroeconomic forces.
- Adjacent market moves.

## Positioning

Define:
- Category: what kind of thing is this?
- Target customer: who specifically?
- Alternative: what do they currently do?
- Key benefit: what they get over the alternative.
- Proof: why believe it.

Test positioning with target customers — does it resonate or slide off?

## Go-to-Market Implications

Good research informs:
- Where customers discover solutions.
- What language resonates.
- Sales motion (self-serve, PLG, sales-led).
- Price points they'll accept.

## Deliverables

A market brief usually contains:
- Market summary (1 page).
- Segments with sizing and characteristics.
- Competitor table.
- Customer quotes supporting claims.
- Positioning statement.
- Risks and open questions.

## Common Pitfalls

- Relying on one-sided friendly interviews.
- Confirming a preferred conclusion.
- Confusing TAM for reachable market.
- Ignoring low-end or "not-really-a-competitor" alternatives.
- Skipping the "why hasn't someone solved this?" question.

## Revisiting

Markets change. Revisit research:
- Before major strategic decisions.
- When growth stalls.
- When new entrants appear.
- At least annually for active markets.
